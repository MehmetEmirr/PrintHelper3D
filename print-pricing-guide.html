How to Price
3D Prints

Complete pricing framework

Pricing 3D prints correctly is one of the biggest challenges for makers and small businesses. Charge too little and you lose money. Charge too much and you lose customers.

1Material Cost

Material cost = (grams ÷ 1000) × price per kg

Get the estimated weight from your slicer (Cura, PrusaSlicer, Bambu Studio). Add 5–10% for support structures.

2Electricity Cost

Electricity = (Watts ÷ 1000) × hours × kWh price

Most FDM desktop printers draw 100–250W. Factor in idle warm-up time too.

3Machine Wear & Maintenance

Nozzles need replacement every 200–500 hours. Bed surfaces wear out. Belts stretch. Add $0.30–$1.00 per print hour as a wear fund.

4Labor & Post-Processing

Include setup time, monitoring, support removal, sanding, painting. Even 10 minutes at $15/hr adds $2.50 to cost.

5Failure Rate Buffer

Add a 5–15% buffer on top of costs to account for failed prints. For new or complex designs, this should be higher.

6Profit Margin

Typical margins range from 30% to 100%+. Custom one-off parts can command 80–150%. Mass-produced items trend toward 30–50%.

Pricing Strategies

Cost-plus pricing: Add a fixed % margin on top of costs. Simple and ensures profitability.

Value-based pricing: Price based on value to the customer. A medical prototype might command 10× the material cost.

Competitive pricing: Match or undercut market rates. Use if competing on Etsy or 3DHubs.
